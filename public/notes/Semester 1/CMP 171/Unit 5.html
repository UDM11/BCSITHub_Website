Unit 5: Data Communication and Computer Network

5.1 Introduction

Data communication involves the transmission of digital data between two or more devices, while a computer network is a collection of interconnected devices that share resources and communicate with each other. These concepts form the backbone of modern connectivity, enabling information exchange across the globe.

  • Purpose: Facilitates data sharing, resource access, and communication between devices.
  • Components: Includes devices (computers, routers), communication media, and protocols.
  • Examples: The Internet, local office networks, and mobile data networks.

5.2 Communication Media

Communication media refers to the physical or wireless channels used to transmit data between devices in a network.

  • Wired Media: Includes twisted pair cables, coaxial cables, and fiber optic cables, offering reliable and high-speed connections.
  • Wireless Media: Uses radio waves, microwaves, or infrared signals, e.g., Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and satellite communication.
  • Examples: Ethernet cables for wired connections and Wi-Fi for wireless networks.

5.3 Communication Mode

Communication modes describe the direction of data flow between devices in a network.

  • Simplex: Data flows in one direction only, e.g., radio broadcasting.
  • Half-Duplex: Data flows in both directions, but not simultaneously, e.g., walkie-talkies.
  • Full-Duplex: Data flows in both directions simultaneously, e.g., telephone conversations or Ethernet connections.

5.4 Computer Network

A computer network is a system of interconnected devices that communicate to share resources, data, and applications.

  • Purpose: Enables resource sharing, communication, and collaboration among users.
  • Components: Consists of nodes (computers, printers), networking hardware (routers, switches), and protocols.
  • Examples: Local Area Networks (LANs) in offices or Wide Area Networks (WANs) like the Internet.

5.5 Advantage and Disadvantage of Computer Network

Computer networks offer numerous benefits but also come with certain challenges.

  • Advantages: Facilitates resource sharing (e.g., printers, files), improves communication, and supports centralized data management.
  • Disadvantages: Requires costly setup and maintenance, poses security risks, and may experience network failures.
  • Examples: Sharing a single printer in an office (advantage) or data breaches due to weak security (disadvantage).

5.6 Types of Networks

Networks are classified based on their geographical coverage and functionality.

  • LAN (Local Area Network): Covers a small area like an office or home, e.g., Wi-Fi networks.
  • WAN (Wide Area Network): Spans large geographical areas, e.g., the Internet or corporate networks.
  • MAN (Metropolitan Area Network): Covers a city or campus, e.g., city-wide fiber networks.
  • PAN (Personal Area Network): Connects personal devices over short distances, e.g., Bluetooth connections.

5.7 Network Topology

Network topology defines the physical or logical arrangement of devices in a network.

  • Bus Topology: All devices share a single communication line, simple but prone to failures.
  • Star Topology: Devices connect to a central hub, reliable but dependent on the hub.
  • Ring Topology: Devices form a closed loop, with data circulating in one direction.
  • Mesh Topology: Devices are interconnected, offering redundancy but complex to implement.

5.8 Communication Protocols (IP, TCP)

Communication protocols are rules that govern data exchange between devices in a network.

  • IP (Internet Protocol): Assigns unique addresses to devices and routes data packets across networks.
  • TCP (Transmission Control Protocol): Ensures reliable data transmission by managing packet order and error checking.
  • Examples: TCP/IP together enable reliable Internet communication, used in web browsing and email.

5.9 Networking Hardware

Networking hardware includes devices that facilitate connectivity and data transfer in a network.

  • Router: Directs data between networks, e.g., connecting a LAN to the Internet.
  • Switch: Connects devices within a network, forwarding data to specific destinations.
  • Access Point: Enables wireless connectivity, e.g., Wi-Fi routers.
  • Network Interface Card (NIC): Allows devices to connect to a network, wired or wireless.

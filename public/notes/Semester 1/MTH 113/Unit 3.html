Unit 3: Derivative

3.1 Derivative

The derivative measures the rate at which a function changes at a specific point, forming a cornerstone of calculus with applications in optimization and modeling.

  • Definition: For a function f(x), the derivative at x = a, denoted f'(a), is the limit lim(h→0) [f(a+h) - f(a)]/h, if it exists.
  • Notation: Derivatives are written as f'(x), dy/dx, or df/dx, representing the instantaneous rate of change of f with respect to x.
  • Geometric Interpretation: The derivative represents the slope of the tangent line to the graph of f(x) at a given point.
  • Applications: Used in physics (e.g., velocity as the derivative of position), economics (e.g., marginal cost), and machine learning (e.g., gradient descent).

The derivative provides a precise tool for analyzing function behavior and solving real-world problems.

3.2 Average Rate of Change

The average rate of change quantifies how a function’s output changes over an interval, serving as a precursor to the instantaneous rate of change.

  • Definition: For a function f(x) over [a, b], the average rate of change is [f(b) - f(a)]/(b - a), representing the slope of the secant line between (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)).
  • Example: If f(x) = x², the average rate of change from x = 1 to x = 3 is [f(3) - f(1)]/(3 - 1) = (9 - 1)/2 = 4.
  • Applications: Used in business to calculate average growth rates (e.g., revenue over time) or in physics for average velocity.
  • Relation to Derivative: As the interval [a, b] shrinks (b → a), the average rate of change approaches the derivative at x = a.

This concept bridges intuitive change measurement with precise derivative calculations.

3.3 Derivative as Slope of the Tangent to Curves

The derivative represents the slope of the tangent line to a curve at a specific point, providing insight into the function’s behavior.

  • Tangent Line: The line that touches the curve at a single point (x, f(x)) and has the same slope as the curve at that point, given by f'(x).
  • Slope Calculation: For f(x) = x², the derivative f'(x) = 2x gives the slope at any point; at x = 2, the slope is f'(2) = 4.
  • Geometric Insight: A positive derivative indicates an upward slope (increasing function), negative indicates downward (decreasing), and zero suggests a horizontal tangent (possible extremum).
  • Applications: Used in optimization to find maxima/minima (e.g., profit maximization) and in graphics to determine curve behavior in rendering algorithms.

The tangent slope interpretation makes derivatives a powerful tool for analyzing curves.

3.4 Methods of Differentiation (Power Rule, Sum Rule, Product Rule, Quotient Rule, Chain Rule)

Differentiation methods provide systematic ways to compute derivatives of various functions, essential for solving complex problems.

  • Power Rule: For f(x) = x^n, f'(x) = nx^(n-1). Example: If f(x) = x³, then f'(x) = 3x².
  • Sum Rule: For f(x) = g(x) + h(x), f'(x) = g'(x) + h'(x). Example: If f(x) = x² + 3x, then f'(x) = 2x + 3.
  • Product Rule: For f(x) = g(x)·h(x), f'(x) = g'(x)·h(x) + g(x)·h'(x). Example: If f(x) = x·sin(x), then f'(x) = sin(x) + x·cos(x).
  • Quotient Rule: For f(x) = g(x)/h(x), f'(x) = [g'(x)·h(x) - g(x)·h'(x)]/[h(x)]². Example: If f(x) = x/(x+1), then f'(x) = 1/(x+1)².
  • Chain Rule: For f(x) = g(h(x)), f'(x) = g'(h(x))·h'(x). Example: If f(x) = (x² + 1)³, then f'(x) = 3(x² + 1)²·2x.

These rules enable efficient derivative calculations for a wide range of functions.

3.5 Differentiation of Implicit and Parametric Functions

Implicit and parametric functions require specialized differentiation techniques, as they are not always expressed as y = f(x).

  • Implicit Differentiation: For equations like x² + y² = 1, differentiate both sides with respect to x, treating y as a function of x (e.g., 2x + 2y·dy/dx = 0, so dy/dx = -x/y).
  • Parametric Differentiation: For functions defined parametrically, e.g., x = t², y = t³, compute dy/dx = (dy/dt)/(dx/dt) = (3t²)/(2t) = 3t/2.
  • Applications: Implicit differentiation is used in economics for constrained optimization (e.g., utility functions); parametric differentiation models motion in physics or animation.
  • Key Considerations: Ensure all terms are differentiated correctly, and solve for dy/dx carefully, checking for domain restrictions.

These techniques extend differentiation to complex relationships in mathematics and applied fields.

3.6 Derivative as Rate of Change, Higher Order Derivatives

The derivative quantifies instantaneous rates of change, and higher order derivatives provide deeper insights into function behavior.

  • Rate of Change: The derivative f'(x) measures how f(x) changes per unit change in x, e.g., velocity (dx/dt) or marginal cost (dC/dq) in business.
  • Higher Order Derivatives: The second derivative f''(x) is the derivative of f'(x), representing the rate of change of the rate of change (e.g., acceleration). Higher orders (f'''(x), etc.) analyze further behavior.
  • Applications: Second derivatives test concavity (f''(x) > 0 implies concave up, f''(x) < 0 implies concave down) for optimization; higher derivatives model complex systems like oscillations.
  • Notation: Higher derivatives are denoted f^(n)(x) or d^n y/dx^n, where n is the order of differentiation.

Higher order derivatives enhance analysis of dynamic systems in physics, economics, and engineering.

Unit 7: Permutations and Combinations

7.1 Basic Principles of Counting, Factorial Notation

The basic principles of counting provide a foundation for calculating the number of possible outcomes in combinatorial problems, with factorial notation as a key tool.

  • Fundamental Counting Principle: If one event can occur in m ways and a second event in n ways, the total number of ways both events can occur is m × n. Example: Choosing a shirt (3 options) and pants (4 options) yields 3 × 4 = 12 outfits.
  • Addition Principle: If an event can occur in m ways or another in n ways, and the ways are mutually exclusive, the total is m + n. Example: Choosing a red (3 options) or blue (2 options) shirt yields 3 + 2 = 5 choices.
  • Factorial Notation: For a positive integer n, n! (n factorial) is the product of all positive integers up to n (e.g., 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120). By definition, 0! = 1.
  • Applications: Used in probability, computer science (e.g., algorithm complexity), and statistics to count possible arrangements or selections.

These principles enable systematic counting of outcomes in complex scenarios.

7.2 Permutation, Permutation of Objects Alike

Permutations deal with the arrangement of objects where order matters, including cases where some objects are identical.

  • Permutation: The number of ways to arrange n distinct objects is n!. Example: Arranging 3 letters (A, B, C) yields 3! = 6 arrangements (ABC, ACB, BAC, etc.).
  • Permutation of r Objects from n: The number of ways to arrange r objects from n distinct objects is P(n,r) = n!/(n-r)!. Example: Arranging 2 out of 4 letters (A, B, C, D) gives P(4,2) = 4!/(4-2)! = 12.
  • Permutation of Objects Alike: For n objects where n₁ are of one type, n₂ of another, etc., the number of distinct arrangements is n!/(n₁!·n₂!·…). Example: Arranging letters in “BOOK” (2 O’s, 1 B, 1 K) gives 4!/(2!·1!·1!) = 24/2 = 12.
  • Applications: Used in scheduling, password generation, and combinatorial design in computer science and operations research.

Permutations account for order-sensitive arrangements, critical for precise counting.

7.3 Permutation with Restrictions, Circular Permutation

Permutations with restrictions and circular permutations address specific constraints or arrangements, common in real-world problems.

  • Permutations with Restrictions: Count arrangements with conditions, such as certain objects being together or apart. Example: Arranging 3 people (A, B, C) with A and B together is calculated by treating A and B as one unit: 2! (for the unit and C) × 2! (for A, B within the unit) = 4.
  • Approach for Restrictions: First satisfy the restriction (e.g., group objects), then arrange remaining elements using standard permutation formulas.
  • Circular Permutation: For n distinct objects arranged in a circle, the number of distinct arrangements is (n-1)!, as rotations are considered identical. Example: Seating 4 people around a table gives (4-1)! = 3! = 6 arrangements.
  • Applications: Used in seating arrangements, network topology design, and scheduling problems where constraints or circularity apply.

These concepts handle specialized arrangement problems with practical relevance.

7.4 Combination, Combination with Restrictions

Combinations focus on selecting objects where order does not matter, including cases with specific constraints.

  • Combination: The number of ways to choose r objects from n distinct objects is C(n,r) = n!/(r!(n-r)!), also denoted as “n choose r.” Example: Choosing 2 out of 4 items (A, B, C, D) gives C(4,2) = 4!/(2!·2!) = 6 (AB, AC, AD, BC, BD, CD).
  • Properties: C(n,r) = C(n,n-r); C(n,0) = C(n,n) = 1. Combinations are used when arrangement is irrelevant, unlike permutations.
  • Combination with Restrictions: Address conditions like including or excluding specific objects. Example: Choosing 3 items from 5 (A, B, C, D, E) with A included reduces to choosing 2 more from 4: C(4,2) = 6.
  • Applications: Used in probability (e.g., lottery odds), data science (e.g., feature selection), and team formation where order is irrelevant.

Combinations simplify counting selections in diverse theoretical and applied contexts.

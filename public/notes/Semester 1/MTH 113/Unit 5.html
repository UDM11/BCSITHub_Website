Unit 5: Integrals

5.1 Indefinite Integral (Algebraic, Exponential, Logarithmic, Trigonometric Functions)

The indefinite integral represents the antiderivative of a function, used to find functions whose derivatives yield the given function, with applications in various fields.

  • Definition: The indefinite integral of f(x), denoted ∫f(x)dx, is a function F(x) such that F'(x) = f(x), plus a constant C (e.g., ∫x²dx = x³/3 + C).
  • Algebraic Functions: For polynomials, use the power rule: ∫x^n dx = x^(n+1)/(n+1) + C, n ≠ -1 (e.g., ∫3x²dx = x³ + C). Rational functions may require partial fractions.
  • Exponential Functions: ∫e^x dx = e^x + C; ∫a^x dx = a^x/ln(a) + C (e.g., ∫2^x dx = 2^x/ln(2) + C). Used in growth and decay models.
  • Logarithmic and Trigonometric Functions: ∫1/x dx = ln|x| + C; ∫sin(x)dx = -cos(x) + C; ∫cos(x)dx = sin(x) + C. These are common in physics and engineering.

Indefinite integrals provide solutions to differential equations and model cumulative quantities.

5.2 Techniques of Integration (Simplification, Substitution, By Parts)

Techniques of integration simplify the process of finding antiderivatives for complex functions, essential for solving diverse mathematical problems.

  • Simplification: Rewrite the integrand to make integration easier (e.g., ∫(x² + 2x)/x dx = ∫(x + 2)dx = x²/2 + 2x + C by dividing each term).
  • Substitution: Change variables to simplify the integral. For ∫f(g(x))g'(x)dx, let u = g(x), so du = g'(x)dx (e.g., ∫2x(x² + 1)dx, let u = x² + 1, du = 2xdx, yields ∫u du = u²/2 + C = (x² + 1)²/2 + C).
  • Integration by Parts: Based on the product rule, ∫u dv = uv - ∫v du. Choose u to simplify when differentiated (e.g., ∫x e^x dx, let u = x, dv = e^x dx, so du = dx, v = e^x, yields x e^x - ∫e^x dx = x e^x - e^x + C).
  • Applications: These techniques solve integrals in physics (e.g., work calculations), economics (e.g., consumer surplus), and engineering (e.g., signal processing).

These methods enable efficient computation of integrals for complex functions.

5.3 Definite Integral

The definite integral quantifies the cumulative effect of a function over an interval, with wide applications in mathematics and applied sciences.

  • Definition: The definite integral of f(x) from a to b, denoted ∫(a to b) f(x)dx, is the limit of Riemann sums, representing the signed area under the curve y = f(x) from x = a to x = b.
  • Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: If F(x) is an antiderivative of f(x), then ∫(a to b) f(x)dx = F(b) - F(a). Example: ∫(1 to 2) x²dx = [x³/3](1 to 2) = 8/3 - 1/3 = 7/3.
  • Geometric Interpretation: For f(x) ≥ 0, the integral is the area under the curve; for f(x) < 0, it contributes negatively to the total area.
  • Applications: Used to calculate areas, volumes, work done (physics), and total profit or cost (economics) over a specific interval.

Definite integrals provide precise measurements of accumulated quantities in real-world scenarios.

5.4 Properties of Definite Integral

Properties of definite integrals simplify calculations and provide insights into their behavior, useful in theoretical and applied contexts.

  • Additivity Over Intervals: ∫(a to c) f(x)dx = ∫(a to b) f(x)dx + ∫(b to c) f(x)dx, where a < b < c, allowing integrals to be split at any point b.
  • Linearity: ∫(a to b) [c·f(x) + k·g(x)]dx = c·∫(a to b) f(x)dx + k·∫(a to b) g(x)dx, where c, k are constants, facilitating combined function integration.
  • Reversal of Limits: ∫(b to a) f(x)dx = -∫(a to b) f(x)dx, meaning reversing the integration bounds negates the integral’s value.
  • Zero Interval: ∫(a to a) f(x)dx = 0, and if f(x) ≥ 0, then ∫(a to b) f(x)dx ≥ 0 for a ≤ b, reflecting area interpretations.

These properties streamline integral computations and enhance understanding of their applications.

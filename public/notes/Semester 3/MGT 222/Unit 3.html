Unit III: Decision Making (7 hrs.)

3.3 Concept of Decision Making

Decision making is the process of choosing among two or more alternatives to achieve a desired outcome. It is the core of management — managers make decisions constantly at every level (strategic, tactical, operational).

  • Decision = Commitment to a course of action
  • Involves: identifying problems/opportunities, gathering information, evaluating alternatives, selecting & implementing the best option, and reviewing results
  • Can be individual or group-based
  • Quality of decisions significantly affects organizational performance
Decision vs Choice
Choice = selecting from given options
Decision = broader process (includes problem identification, analysis, implementation)

3.5 The Decision Making Process

  1. Identify the problem / opportunity
    Gap between current & desired state
    Symptoms vs root causes (use 5-Why, Fishbone diagram)
  2. Gather relevant information
    Facts, data, stakeholder views
    Internal & external sources
  3. Identify alternatives
    Brainstorm creatively
    Avoid premature judgment
  4. Weigh evidence / evaluate alternatives
    Criteria: cost, feasibility, risk, time, alignment with goals
    Quantitative (e.g., NPV) or qualitative methods
  5. Choose among alternatives
    Select best option (maximizing, satisficing)
  6. Implement the decision
    Action plan, resource allocation, communication
  7. Review & evaluate results
    Feedback loop → learn for future decisions
    Adjust if necessary
This is the classical rational model (Simon, 1960).
Real-world decisions often deviate due to time pressure, incomplete information, politics, etc.

3.6 Approaches in Decision Making

Approach Key Thinker(s) Core Idea Assumptions / Features Strengths Limitations
Rationality / Rational Model Herbert Simon (early) Choose option that maximizes value Complete information, clear goals, unlimited time & capacity Logical, systematic Unrealistic in practice
Bounded Rationality Herbert Simon (1957) Satisficing – choose “good enough” option Limited information, time, cognitive capacity More realistic May miss optimal solution
Intuition Daniel Kahneman, Gary Klein Rapid, unconscious pattern recognition Experience-based, fast, emotion-involved Effective in familiar, fast-paced situations Subject to bias, hard to explain
Evidence-Based Management Jeffrey Pfeffer, Robert Sutton (2006) Decisions based on best available scientific evidence Like evidence-based medicine — use research, data, not fads Reduces fads & myths Evidence sometimes incomplete or conflicting
Crowdsourcing James Surowiecki (Wisdom of Crowds, 2004) Leverage collective intelligence Diverse, independent opinions + aggregation Can outperform experts (prediction markets, open innovation) Groupthink, polarization, manipulation risk

3.7 Decision Making Biases and Errors

Humans systematically deviate from rationality — these are called cognitive biases.

Bias / Error Description Example How to Reduce
Confirmation Bias Seek/interpret information that confirms beliefs Ignoring negative feedback about a favored strategy Actively seek disconfirming evidence
Anchoring Bias Rely too heavily on first piece of information Initial salary offer anchors final negotiation Consider multiple reference points
Overconfidence Bias Overestimate own accuracy/judgment CEO believes merger will succeed despite risks Use base rates, pre-mortems
Availability Heuristic Judge likelihood by ease of recall Overestimate plane crash risk after news coverage Use statistics, not vivid examples
Sunk Cost Fallacy Continue because of past investment Keep funding failing project “because we’ve spent so much” Ignore sunk costs; focus on future costs/benefits
Framing Effect Decision changes based on how options are presented “90% success rate” vs “10% failure rate” Reframe problem in multiple ways
Groupthink Group prioritizes consensus over critical evaluation Challenger space shuttle disaster Devil’s advocate, anonymous input
Biases are universal — even experienced managers fall victim. Awareness + structured processes help mitigate them.

3.8 Cutting-edge Approaches for Improving Decision Making

  • Behavioral Economics & Nudge Theory (Thaler & Sunstein)
    Design choice architecture to guide better decisions without restricting freedom (default options, simplification)
  • Data-driven / AI-supported Decisions
    Big data, predictive analytics, machine learning, decision support systems, recommendation engines
  • Pre-mortems & Red Teaming
    Imagine failure before decision → identify risks early (Gary Klein)
  • Scenario Planning & War Gaming
    Shell Oil pioneered — multiple plausible futures → robust strategies
  • Agile & Adaptive Decision Making
    Short feedback loops, experimentation, pivoting (startup methodology)
  • Algorithmic Decision Making & Explainable AI
    Algorithms for credit scoring, hiring, medical diagnosis — but need transparency & fairness
  • Decision Quality Frameworks (e.g., SDG – Strategic Decision Group)
    Structured dialogue, clear criteria, trade-off analysis
  • Cognitive Diversity in Teams
    Diverse perspectives reduce blind spots & groupthink
Modern decision making combines human judgment with data, technology, structured processes, and awareness of psychological traps.

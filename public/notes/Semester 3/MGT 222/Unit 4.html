4.6 Definition, Importance, and Types of Planning

Planning is the management function that involves setting objectives and deciding how best to achieve them. It is often called the primary management function because all other functions (organizing, leading, controlling) depend on it.

Importance of Planning

  • Provides direction & reduces uncertainty
  • Minimizes waste of resources (time, money, effort)
  • Establishes standards for controlling performance
  • Facilitates coordination across departments
  • Encourages innovation & proactive thinking
  • Helps in risk identification & mitigation
  • Improves decision-making by providing framework

Types of Planning

Basis Type Time Horizon Scope / Level Examples
Time Horizon Strategic Planning 3–10 years Top level / Organization-wide Vision/mission, new market entry, diversification
Tactical / Functional Planning 1–3 years Middle level / Departmental Annual budget, marketing plan, HR recruitment plan
Operational / Short-range Planning Days – 1 year Lower level / Day-to-day Daily production schedule, shift roster, weekly sales target
Scope / Specificity Directional Planning Flexible guidelines Dynamic environments “Increase market share” without rigid targets
Specific Planning Detailed & measurable Stable environments “Achieve 15% sales growth in FY 2082/83”
Frequency Single-use / Standing Plans One-time / Unique situations Program, project, budget New product launch program
Standing / Repeated-use Plans Ongoing / Recurring Policies, procedures, rules Leave policy, recruitment procedure

4.7 The Planning Process

  1. Establishing Objectives / Setting Goals
    Clear, measurable, time-bound (SMART goals)
    Hierarchy: Strategic → Tactical → Operational
  2. Determining Planning Premises
    Assumptions about future (internal & external environment)
    Forecasting: economic, technological, legal, social trends
  3. Identifying Alternative Courses of Action
    Brainstorming, creativity techniques
    More alternatives → better chance of good solution
  4. Evaluating Alternatives
    Quantitative: Cost-benefit, NPV, payback period
    Qualitative: Risk, ethics, feasibility, alignment
  5. Selecting the Best Alternative
    Maximizing vs Satisficing approach
  6. Formulating Derivative Plans
    Supporting plans (budgets, schedules, procedures)
  7. Implementing the Plan
    Communication, resource allocation, motivation
  8. Review & Control
    Compare actual vs planned
    Take corrective action → planning is continuous
Planning is not a one-time event — it is an ongoing, iterative process.

4.8 Contingency Factors in Planning

Contingency approach: “It depends” — planning effectiveness depends on several situational factors.

Key Contingency Factors

Organization Size Number of employees, complexity Large organizations → more formal, detailed planning Ncell vs small cyber café

4.9 Contemporary Issues in Planning

  • Planning in Dynamic & Turbulent Environments
    Traditional long-term planning less effective → agile planning, rolling forecasts, scenario planning
  • Technology & Big Data
    AI-driven forecasting, predictive analytics, real-time dashboards
  • Sustainability & ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance)
    Planning must integrate climate goals, ethical sourcing, diversity
  • Globalization & Geopolitical Risks
    Supply chain disruptions, trade wars, currency fluctuations
  • Employee Involvement & Bottom-up Planning
    Participative planning improves buy-in & accuracy
  • Over-planning / Analysis Paralysis
    Too much detail stifles action — balance is key
  • Ethical & Social Responsibility
    Plans must consider societal impact, not just profit
Modern planning emphasizes flexibility, agility, scenario thinking, and stakeholder inclusion.

4.10 Concept of Organizing and Its Basic Elements

Organizing is the management function that arranges people, resources, tasks, and authority to accomplish objectives efficiently.

Basic Elements of Organizing

  • Division of Work / Specialization
    Breaking tasks into smaller, specialized jobs (Adam Smith’s pin factory example)
  • Departmentalization
    Grouping jobs into departments (functional, product, geographic, customer, process)
  • Chain of Command / Hierarchy
    Clear line of authority from top to bottom
  • Span of Control
    Number of subordinates a manager can effectively supervise (narrow vs wide)
  • Centralization vs Decentralization
    Degree to which decision-making is concentrated at top or delegated
  • Formalization
    Extent of written rules, procedures, job descriptions

4.11 Traditional Organizational Designs

Design Structure Strengths Weaknesses Suitable For
Simple Structure Owner/manager + few employees Fast decisions, low cost Overload on owner, informal Small businesses, startups
Functional Structure Grouped by function (marketing, finance, production) Specialization, efficiency Silo mentality, slow coordination Stable, medium-size firms
Divisional Structure Grouped by product, region, customer Focus, accountability Duplication of resources Large, diversified firms
Matrix Structure Functional + project/product managers (dual authority) Flexibility, resource sharing Power struggle, confusion Project-based, dynamic firms
Line & Staff Structure Line (decision) + staff (advisory) Expert advice without losing control Staff-line conflict Manufacturing, government

4.12 Organizing Flexibility in the Twenty-first Century

Modern organizations must be adaptable due to globalization, technology, competition, and uncertainty.

Contemporary Flexible Designs

  • Team-based Structure
    Cross-functional, self-managed teams
  • Boundaryless Organization
    Remove internal & external barriers (Jack Welch – GE)
  • Virtual / Network Organization
    Core firm + outsourced partners, freelancers, gig economy
  • Learning Organization
    Continuous learning, knowledge sharing (Peter Senge)
  • Agile Organization
    Scrum, sprints, iterative planning (from software development to business)
  • Ambidextrous Organization
    Balance exploitation (current business) & exploration (innovation)
  • Holacracy & Self-management
    No fixed hierarchy – roles & circles (Zappos experiment)
Key trends: flatter hierarchies, empowerment, digital tools, remote work, adaptability to change.

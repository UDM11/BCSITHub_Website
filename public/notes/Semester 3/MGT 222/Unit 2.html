2.7 The Historical Context of Management

Management as a formal discipline is relatively young (~late 19th – early 20th century), but organized human activity and coordination of resources have existed for thousands of years.

~3000–2500 BCE

Egyptian pyramids: planning, organizing large workforce, resource allocation, supervision

~500 BCE

Sun Tzu – The Art of War: strategy, leadership, deception, adaptability

~400 BCE

Xenophon & Plato: division of labor, specialization

~1400–1500 CE

Venetian Arsenal: early mass production, assembly-line concept

1776

Adam Smith – Wealth of Nations: division of labor, productivity gains

Industrial Revolution (1760–1840)

Factories → need for systematic management of workers, machines, time

Late 19th – early 20th century

Birth of modern management theories (Taylor, Fayol, Weber)

Management thought evolved mainly in response to industrialization, large organizations, and the need to improve efficiency and productivity.

2.8 The Classical Management Theory

Focus: Efficiency, structure, rationality, universal principles

Three main branches

Approach Key Contributor Year(s) Core Focus Main Principles / Concepts
Scientific Management Frederick W. Taylor 1890s–1910s Work efficiency at shop-floor level • Time & motion study
• One best way
• Piece-rate pay
• Selection & training of workers
• Separation of planning & doing
Administrative / Process Management Henri Fayol 1916 (General & Industrial Management) Top-level management functions 14 Principles of Management
5 Functions: Planning, Organizing, Commanding, Coordinating, Controlling (POCCC)
Bureaucratic Management Max Weber 1920s (published posthumously) Ideal organizational structure • Hierarchy of authority
• Division of labor
• Written rules & procedures
• Impersonality
• Merit-based selection & promotion
Strength: Provided foundation for modern organizations
Criticism: Ignored human emotions, treated workers as machines, rigid, not suitable for dynamic environments

2.9 The Behavioral Management Theories

Shift in focus: from tasks & structures → people, motivation, group behavior, leadership

Major Contributions

Contributor / Study Period Key Insight / Theory Main Message
Hawthorne Studies (Elton Mayo et al.) 1924–1932 Hawthorne Effect Attention & social factors improve productivity more than physical conditions
Human Relations Movement 1930s–1950s Social needs are important at work Informal groups, morale, communication affect performance
Abraham Maslow 1943 Hierarchy of Needs Physiological → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-actualization
Douglas McGregor 1960 Theory X vs Theory Y X = workers dislike work & need control
Y = workers are self-motivated & seek responsibility
Frederick Herzberg 1959 Two-Factor Theory (Motivation-Hygiene) Hygiene factors prevent dissatisfaction
Motivators create satisfaction
Behavioral theories laid foundation for modern HR practices, leadership styles, organizational culture, motivation theories, and employee engagement.

2.10 The Quantitative Management Theory

Also called Management Science / Operations Research approach

Origin: World War II → military operations research (logistics, resource allocation, bombing patterns)

Core Ideas

  • Use of mathematical models, statistics, and optimization techniques
  • Focus on decision-making under certainty, risk, or uncertainty
  • Tools: Linear Programming, Queuing Theory, Simulation, Decision Trees, Inventory Models (EOQ), PERT/CPM

Major Areas

  • Management Science / Operations Research
  • Decision Theory
  • Systems Analysis
Very powerful for structured, repetitive, quantifiable problems (production scheduling, transportation, facility location, inventory control).
Limitation: Difficult to apply to behavioral/human elements or highly uncertain/creative situations.

2.11 Contemporary Management Theories

A. Systems Theory

  • Organization is an open system interacting with environment
  • Key concepts: Inputs → Transformation Process → Outputs → Feedback
  • Synergy: whole > sum of parts
  • Subsystems must work together (interdependence)
  • Contributors: Ludwig von Bertalanffy (General Systems Theory), Katz & Kahn

B. Contingency Theory

  • There is no one best way to manage
  • Best structure/leadership/style depends on situational factors
  • Key contingencies: environment (stable vs dynamic), technology, size, strategy, culture
  • Contributors: Joan Woodward (technology), Paul Lawrence & Jay Lorsch (environment), Fred Fiedler (leadership style)

C. Dynamic Capabilities Theory

  • Organizations must sense, seize, and transform/reconfigure resources to remain competitive in rapidly changing environments
  • Focus: adaptability, innovation, learning
  • Key contributors: David Teece (1997), Eisenhardt & Martin
  • Especially relevant in digital age, disruptive technologies, globalization
Contemporary theories emphasize flexibility, adaptability, learning, environment-organization fit, and continuous renewal rather than rigid universal principles.

Unit V: Leadership (7 hrs.)

5.1 The Meaning of Leadership

Leadership is the process of influencing others to understand and agree about what needs to be done and how it can be done effectively, and the process of facilitating individual and collective efforts to accomplish shared objectives.

  • Leadership = Influence + Direction + Motivation + Goal achievement
  • Occurs at all levels (not just top management)
  • Involves both formal (position-based) and informal (personal influence) authority
  • Requires followers — no followers, no leader
Key elements of leadership definition:
• Influence (not coercion)
• Voluntary followership
• Shared goals
• Change & direction

5.2 Management versus Leadership

Management and leadership are complementary but distinct roles. Organizations need both.

Dimension Management Leadership
Focus Things / Processes / Stability People / Vision / Change
Primary activity Planning, organizing, controlling Inspiring, motivating, directing
Power source Position / Authority Personal influence / Charisma / Trust
Orientation Short-term, efficiency, doing things right Long-term, effectiveness, doing the right things
Approach to people Administers, controls, maintains Innovates, develops, inspires
Outcome Predictability, order, consistency Change, growth, alignment
Classic quote “Managers do things right; leaders do the right things.” — Warren Bennis “Leadership is lifting a person’s vision to higher sights…” — Peter Drucker
Effective managers are often good leaders, but not all leaders are good managers and vice versa.
Modern organizations need both — “leader-managers”.

5.3 Leadership and Power

Power = the ability to influence others and get them to do what you want.

French & Raven’s Five Bases of Power (1959, later expanded)

Power Base Source How it works Long-term effectiveness Typical user
Legitimate Position / Formal authority “I have the right to ask because of my role” Moderate Managers, supervisors
Reward Ability to give rewards “Do this and you’ll get bonus/promotion” Moderate (can create dependency) Managers with budget control
Coercive Ability to punish “Do this or face penalty” Low (creates resentment) Authoritarian leaders
Expert Knowledge / Skill “Follow me because I know best” High Technical experts, consultants
Referent Charisma / Admiration / Respect “I want to be like / please this person” Very high Charismatic / Transformational leaders
Best leaders combine expert and referent power with legitimate power — coercive & pure reward power are least sustainable.

5.4 Early Leadership Theories: Trait Theories and Behavioral Theories

A. Trait Theories (1900s–1940s)

“Leaders are born, not made”

  • Focus: Personal qualities / characteristics that distinguish leaders from non-leaders
  • Common traits identified: Intelligence, self-confidence, determination, integrity, sociability, charisma, drive
  • Stogdill’s review (1948 & 1974): Some traits matter, but situation also important → led to decline of pure trait theory

B. Behavioral Theories (1940s–1960s)

“Leaders are made, not born” — focus on what leaders do rather than who they are.

Study / Theory Institution Two Main Leadership Behaviors Key Finding

Behavioral theories showed leadership can be learned → training programs possible.
Limitation: No universal “best” style → led to contingency theories.

5.5 Contingency Theories of Leadership

“It depends” — the best leadership style depends on the situation.

Hersey and Blanchard’s Situational Leadership Theory (1977)

  • Leader adjusts style based on followers’ readiness / maturity level
  • Readiness = Ability + Willingness
  • Four styles:
    • S1 – Telling / Directing (high task, low relationship)
    • S2 – Selling / Coaching (high task, high relationship)
    • S3 – Participating / Supporting (low task, high relationship)
    • S4 – Delegating (low task, low relationship)

Path-Goal Theory (Robert House, 1971 & 1996)

  • Leader’s job: Help followers achieve goals & provide support
  • Leader clarifies path, removes obstacles, increases rewards
  • Four leadership styles:
    • Directive
    • Supportive
    • Participative
    • Achievement-oriented
  • Choice of style depends on:
    • Follower characteristics (experience, locus of control)
    • Task/environmental factors (structure, ambiguity)
Both theories are situational — no single best style; effective leaders adapt.

Theory / Approach Key Contributor(s) Core Idea Main Characteristics

Transactional Leadership Bass Exchange-based (rewards & corrections) Contingent reward, Management by exception

Contemporary theories emphasize inspiration, ethics, authenticity, service, and emotional connection — especially important in knowledge-based, volatile environments.

5.7 Emotional Intelligence in Leadership

Emotional Intelligence (EI) = ability to recognize, understand, manage, and influence one’s own emotions and the emotions of others.

Daniel Goleman’s EI Model (most popular in leadership)

  • Self-Awareness: Know your emotions, strengths, weaknesses
  • Self-Regulation: Control disruptive emotions, stay calm
  • Motivation: Drive to achieve beyond external rewards
  • Empathy: Understand others’ feelings, perspectives
  • Social Skills: Build rapport, manage relationships

Why EI Matters in Leadership

  • Builds trust & loyalty
  • Improves team morale & collaboration
  • Handles conflict & stress better
  • Inspires & motivates diverse teams
  • Critical in transformational & servant leadership
Goleman (1998): EI is more important than IQ or technical skills for leadership success — especially at higher levels.

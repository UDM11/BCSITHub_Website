8.1 The Strategic Importance of Information for Management

In today’s digital economy, **information** is considered a strategic resource — comparable to capital, labor, and raw materials. Effective management of information creates competitive advantage, supports decision-making, and enables organizational survival and growth.

Why Information is Strategically Important

  • Foundation for Decision Making
    Accurate, timely information reduces uncertainty and improves quality of strategic, tactical, and operational decisions
  • Competitive Advantage (Porter’s View)
    • Cost leadership — efficient operations through real-time data
    • Differentiation — personalized services, innovation via analytics
    • Focus — deep understanding of niche markets
  • Enables Agility & Responsiveness
    Fast detection of market changes, customer preferences, competitor moves
  • Supports Value Chain Activities
    Inbound logistics → operations → outbound logistics → marketing & sales → service (all improved by IT)
  • Facilitates Globalization
    24/7 coordination across geographies, virtual teams, e-commerce
  • Risk Management & Compliance
    Fraud detection, cybersecurity monitoring, regulatory reporting
  • Innovation & New Business Models
    Platforms (e.g., Daraz, Foodmandu), data monetization, AI-driven products
In Nepal: Companies like Ncell, NTC, Chaudhary Group, IME, and banks (Nabil, Global IME) use information systems for customer insights, mobile banking, supply chain tracking, and digital payments.

8.2 Characteristics and Costs of Useful Information

Characteristics of Useful / Good Information (GIGO Principle – Garbage In, Garbage Out)

Characteristic Meaning Why It Matters
Accuracy Free from errors Wrong data → wrong decisions
Timeliness Available when needed Outdated info loses value (e.g., stock price)
Relevance Pertains to the decision at hand Avoids information overload
Completeness No missing parts Partial data leads to partial understanding
Conciseness Only necessary detail Reduces cognitive load
Reliability Trustworthy source Credible data builds confidence
Cost-effectiveness Value > cost of obtaining Avoid expensive but useless information
Understandability Clear to intended user Technical jargon reduces usability

Costs of Useful Information

  • Acquisition Cost – collecting raw data (sensors, surveys, purchase)
  • Processing Cost – cleaning, transforming, analyzing
  • Storage Cost – servers, cloud, backups
  • Transmission Cost – network bandwidth, security
  • Opportunity Cost – time & money not spent elsewhere
  • Security & Compliance Cost – encryption, GDPR-like compliance
  • Human Cost – training, analysts, IT staff
Managers must balance the cost of information against its strategic value — too much information can be as harmful as too little.

8.3 Capturing, Processing, and Protecting Information

Capturing Information

  • Internal sources: ERP, CRM, POS, employee reports
  • External sources: market research, social media, government data, competitors
  • Methods: sensors/IoT, web scraping, APIs, manual entry, customer feedback

Processing Information

  • Steps: Collection → Validation → Transformation → Analysis → Presentation
  • Tools: ETL (Extract, Transform, Load), Data warehouses, BI tools (Power BI, Tableau), AI/ML algorithms
  • Output forms: Reports, dashboards, predictive models, alerts

Protecting Information

Threat Protection Method Examples / Tools
Unauthorized access Authentication & Authorization User ID/password, MFA, role-based access control (RBAC)
Data interception Encryption TLS/SSL (in transit), AES (at rest)
Data loss / corruption Backup & Recovery 3-2-1 backup rule, RAID, cloud backups
Malware / Ransomware Antivirus, firewalls, endpoint protection Regular patching, zero-trust architecture
Insider threats Auditing & Monitoring SIEM tools, DLP (Data Loss Prevention)
In Nepal: Cyberattacks on banks (e.g., NIC Asia 2020 incident) show weak protection leads to huge financial & reputational loss.

8.4 Issues Managing Information System

Major Contemporary Challenges

  • Data Overload / Information Overload
    Too much data → difficulty finding relevant insights → decision paralysis
  • Data Quality & Integrity
    Inaccurate, incomplete, duplicate data → garbage in, garbage out
  • Privacy & Ethical Concerns
    GDPR-like regulations, customer data misuse, surveillance capitalism
  • Cybersecurity Threats
    Ransomware, phishing, data breaches, supply-chain attacks
  • Integration of Legacy & New Systems
    Old core banking vs modern mobile apps — compatibility issues
  • Cost vs Benefit Justification
    High IT investment (ERP, cloud, AI) → ROI difficult to measure
  • Skill Gap & Change Management
    Employees resist new systems; lack of digital literacy
  • Cloud vs On-premise Dilemma
    Security & control vs scalability & cost
  • AI & Algorithmic Bias
    Automated decisions may discriminate if training data is biased
Successful IS management requires alignment with business strategy, strong governance, continuous training, robust security, and ethical frameworks.

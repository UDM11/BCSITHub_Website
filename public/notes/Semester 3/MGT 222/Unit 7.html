Unit VII: Controlling (6 hrs.)

7.1 Concept and Basic Elements of Control

Controlling is the management function that ensures actual performance aligns with planned performance. It involves monitoring, measuring, comparing results against standards, and taking corrective action when deviations occur.

Controlling = Ensuring what should happen does happen

Basic Elements / Components of Control Process

  1. Establishing standards / performance criteria
    Quantitative (sales target = 500 units/month) or qualitative (customer satisfaction score ≥ 4.5/5)
  2. Measuring actual performance
    Through reports, observation, audits, KPIs, dashboards
  3. Comparing actual performance with standards
    Identify deviations (positive or negative)
  4. Taking corrective action
    If deviation exists → adjust resources, processes, training, or revise standards
Controlling is both forward-looking (prevents problems) and backward-looking (analyzes past performance).

7.2 The Nature of Control: Purpose, Types, and Steps

Purpose / Objectives of Control

  • Ensure goal achievement
  • Minimize deviations & waste
  • Facilitate coordination
  • Improve future planning
  • Provide feedback for learning
  • Support decentralization (empower lower levels with clear standards)

Types of Control

Type Timing Focus Examples Strength
Feedforward / Preventive Control Before activity Anticipate & prevent problems Employee training, supplier quality checks, budgeting Most proactive & cost-effective
Concurrent / Screening Control During activity Real-time monitoring & adjustment Immediate correction
Feedback / Post-action Control After activity Learn from results Annual performance appraisal, financial audit, customer feedback analysis Helps future planning

Steps in the Control Process (Detailed)

  1. Set performance standards
    Must be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound (SMART)
  2. Measure actual performance
    Use reliable, timely, accurate data sources
  3. Compare actual vs standard
    Identify variance (positive = good, negative = problem)
  4. Take corrective action
    • Do nothing (if within tolerance)
    • Correct deviation (adjust resources/processes)
    • Revise standards (if unrealistic)

7.3 Operations Control

Focuses on day-to-day production/service delivery processes.

Common Operations Control Techniques

  • Budgetary Control – compare actual vs budgeted expenses
  • Statistical Process Control (SPC) – control charts, Six Sigma
  • Quality Control – inspection, TQM, ISO standards
  • Inventory Control – EOQ, JIT, ABC analysis
  • Production Scheduling & Control – Gantt charts, PERT/CPM
  • Ratio Analysis – productivity ratios, capacity utilization
Operations control is usually concurrent or feedback-oriented and heavily uses quantitative tools.

7.4 Financial Control

Ensures effective & efficient use of financial resources.

Major Financial Control Tools

  • Budgets – master budget, cash budget, capital budget
  • Financial Ratio Analysis
    • Liquidity: current ratio, quick ratio
    • Profitability: ROA, ROE, profit margin
    • Leverage: debt-equity ratio
    • Activity: inventory turnover, receivables turnover
  • Standard Costing & Variance Analysis – material, labor, overhead variances
  • Break-even Analysis – determine sales volume needed to cover costs
  • Auditing – internal & external (financial & operational)
Financial controls are mostly feedback-oriented and critical for survival & profitability.

7.5 Structural Control

Control achieved through organizational structure, policies, rules, procedures, hierarchy, and culture.

Mechanisms

  • Bureaucratic Control – rules, procedures, hierarchy, formal authority
  • Clan Control – shared values, norms, culture (common in startups, professional firms)
  • Market Control – price mechanisms, competition, profit centers
  • Output / Results Control – focus on measurable outcomes (MBO, KPIs)
Structural control is preventive (feedforward) and works best when combined with other types.

7.6 Strategic Control

Ensures organization is moving toward its strategic goals in a changing environment.

Types / Focus Areas

  • Premises Control – monitor validity of planning assumptions
  • Implementation Control – monitor progress of strategic plans (milestones, budgets)
  • Strategic Surveillance – broad scanning for unexpected threats/opportunities
  • Special Alert Control – rapid response to sudden crises

Tools

  • Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton)
  • Strategy Maps
  • Environmental Scanning
  • Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) aligned with strategy
Strategic control is long-term, proactive, and future-oriented.

7.7 Managing Control in Organizations

Key Principles for Effective Control Systems

  • Timeliness – information must be available soon enough to act
  • Accuracy – reliable data
  • Relevance – focus on critical success factors
  • Flexibility – adapt to changes
  • Economy – cost of control < benefit
  • Understandability – clear to users
  • Acceptability – employees should perceive system as fair
  • Multiple Criteria – avoid over-emphasis on single measure

Challenges in Managing Control

  • Resistance to control (perceived as distrust)
  • Over-control → stifles creativity
  • Under-control → chaos
  • Goal displacement (focus on measurable indicators, ignore others)
  • Short-termism (sacrifice long-term for short-term targets)

7.8 Contemporary Issues in Controlling

  • Control in Remote & Virtual Work
    Outcome-based vs activity monitoring, trust vs surveillance tools
  • Big Data, AI & Analytics in Control
    Real-time dashboards, predictive control, anomaly detection
  • Balanced Scorecard & Strategy Maps
    Multi-dimensional performance measurement
  • Ethical & Sustainability Control
    ESG metrics, ethical audits, whistleblower systems
  • Control in Gig & Flexible Workforce
    Platform ratings, peer reviews, self-regulation
  • Cybersecurity & Data Control
    Access controls, encryption, audit trails
  • Agile Control Systems
    Short feedback loops, iterative goals, adaptive targets
Modern control is shifting from rigid, top-down systems toward flexible, trust-based, data-driven, and multi-stakeholder approaches.

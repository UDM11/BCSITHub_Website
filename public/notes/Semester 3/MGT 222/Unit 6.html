Unit VI: Motivation (6 hrs.)

6.1 Concept of Motivation

Motivation is the set of forces that initiates, directs, and sustains behavior toward a goal. It explains why people behave the way they do at work.

  • Motivation = Direction + Intensity + Persistence
  • Key question for managers: “How can I get employees to do what I want them to do, willingly and enthusiastically?”
  • Motivation links to performance, job satisfaction, retention, productivity, creativity, and organizational citizenship behavior
Motivation is not the same as: • Ability (skills & knowledge) • Opportunity (resources & environment) Performance = f (Ability × Motivation × Opportunity)

6.2 Early Theories of Motivation

Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs Theory (1943, 1954)

People are motivated by five levels of needs arranged in a hierarchy. Lower needs must be satisfied before higher ones become motivators.

  1. Physiological needs – food, water, shelter, salary
  2. Safety needs – security, stability, protection, job security
  3. Social / Belongingness needs – friendship, love, group acceptance, team spirit
  4. Esteem needs – self-respect, recognition, status, achievement
  5. Self-actualization needs – realizing full potential, personal growth
Criticisms: Little empirical support, rigid hierarchy not universal, cultural differences, people pursue multiple needs simultaneously.

McGregor’s Theory X and Theory Y (1960)

Assumption Theory X (Traditional / Pessimistic) Theory Y (Modern / Optimistic)
Attitude toward work Dislike work, avoid it if possible Work is natural, like play or rest
Motivation Need external control, threats, rewards Self-directed & self-controlled
Responsibility Avoid responsibility, prefer direction Seek responsibility under right conditions
Creativity Little creativity High creativity & imagination
Management style implied Autocratic, close supervision Participative, empowering

Herzberg’s Two-Factor Theory (Motivation-Hygiene Theory, 1959)

  • Hygiene Factors (Dissatisfiers) – prevent dissatisfaction but do not motivate
    Salary, company policy, supervision, working conditions, job security, status, interpersonal relations
  • Motivators (Satisfiers) – true motivators when present
    Achievement, recognition, work itself, responsibility, advancement, growth
Implication: Fixing hygiene factors stops complaints but does not create high motivation. Real motivation comes from job content (motivators).

6.3 Contemporary Theories in Motivation

Goal-Setting Theory (Edwin Locke & Gary Latham, 1968–present)

  • Specific & difficult goals lead to higher performance than easy or vague goals
  • Key elements:
    • Specific & challenging goals
    • Feedback on progress
    • Commitment to goal
    • Task complexity (more important for simple tasks)
    • Cultural context (individualistic vs collectivist)

Reinforcement Theory (B.F. Skinner – Operant Conditioning)

  • Behavior is a function of its consequences
  • Types of reinforcement:
    • Positive reinforcement – reward desirable behavior
    • Negative reinforcement – remove unpleasant consequence
    • Punishment – apply unpleasant consequence
    • Extinction – withhold reward
  • Used in performance appraisal, incentive systems

Equity Theory (J. Stacy Adams, 1963)

People compare their input-outcome ratio with others’ ratios. Perceived inequity creates tension → motivation to restore equity.

  • Inputs: effort, skills, experience
  • Outcomes: pay, recognition, benefits
  • Responses to inequity: change inputs, change outcomes, distort perceptions, quit

Expectancy Theory (Victor Vroom, 1964)

Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence

  • Expectancy: Belief that effort leads to performance
  • Instrumentality: Belief that performance leads to reward
  • Valence: Value placed on the reward
Contemporary theories are more process-oriented and situational than early content theories.

6.4 Contemporary Issues of Motivation

  • Motivating a Diverse Workforce
    Cultural differences (collectivist vs individualist), generational differences (Baby Boomers, Gen X, Millennials, Gen Z), gender, remote workers
  • Motivating Contingent / Gig Workers
    Freelancers, temporary staff – focus on intrinsic rewards, flexibility, recognition
  • Motivating in Virtual / Remote Environments
    Challenges: isolation, lack of visibility; solutions: regular check-ins, virtual team-building, clear goals
  • Work-Life Balance & Well-being
    Flexible hours, wellness programs, mental health support
  • Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Motivation
    Over-rewarding can reduce intrinsic motivation (overjustification effect)
  • Motivation in Knowledge-Based & Creative Jobs
    Autonomy, mastery, purpose (Daniel Pink – Drive)
  • Sustainability & Purpose-Driven Motivation
    Linking work to social/environmental impact increases engagement
  • AI & Automation Impact
    Fear of job loss → need for reskilling motivation
Modern motivation emphasizes purpose, autonomy, fairness, well-being, and alignment with personal values — especially among younger generations.

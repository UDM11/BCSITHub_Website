3.1 Terminology and Definition of Probability

Probability is a numerical measure of the likelihood that a particular event will occur. It ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

Basic Terminology

  • Experiment / Random Experiment: Any process that produces uncertain outcomes (e.g., tossing a coin, rolling a die, loading a webpage)
  • Outcome: A single possible result of an experiment
  • Sample Space (S): Set of all possible outcomes (denoted by S or Ω)
    Example: Coin toss → S = {Head, Tail}
    Die roll → S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}
  • Event: Any subset of sample space (denoted by capital letters A, B, C…)
    Simple event: single outcome
    Compound event: more than one outcome
  • Mutually Exclusive Events: Cannot occur at the same time (A ∩ B = ∅)
  • Exhaustive Events: Cover the entire sample space (A ∪ B ∪ … = S)
  • Independent Events: Occurrence of one does not affect the other
  • Complementary Event: A̅ (not A) → P(A̅) = 1 – P(A)

3.2 Addition and Multiplication Law

Addition Law / Rule of Addition

For any two events A and B:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) – P(A ∩ B)
  • Mutually exclusive events (A ∩ B = ∅): P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)
  • Exhaustive & mutually exclusive: P(A) + P(B) + … = 1

Multiplication Law / Rule of Multiplication

For any two events A and B:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A) or P(B) × P(A|B)
  • Independent events (occurrence of one does not affect the other):
    P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
    Also P(A|B) = P(A) and P(B|A) = P(B)
  • Dependent events: Use conditional probability
A card is drawn from a standard deck (52 cards). Find probability of drawing:
a) King or Queen (mutually exclusive) → 4/52 + 4/52 = 8/52 = 2/13
b) King and then Queen (without replacement, dependent) → (4/52) × (4/51) = 16/2652 ≈ 0.006
c) King and Queen (with replacement, independent) → (4/52) × (4/52) = 16/2704 ≈ 0.0059

3.3 Conditional Probability and Bayes’ Theorem related to IT

Conditional Probability

Probability of event A occurring given that event B has already occurred.

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B) (provided P(B) > 0)

Bayes’ Theorem (Thomas Bayes, 1763)

Allows us to reverse conditional probabilities — very important in IT (spam filtering, medical diagnosis, fraud detection, machine learning).

P(A|B) = [P(B|A) × P(A)] / P(B)

Where P(B) = P(B|A) × P(A) + P(B|A̅) × P(A̅) (total probability rule)

Real IT Applications of Bayes’ Theorem

Application What is being calculated P(A) P(B|A) P(B|A̅)
Spam Email Filter P(Spam | contains “win lottery”) Prior probability of spam Likelihood word appears in spam Likelihood in non-spam
Network Intrusion Detection P(Attack | unusual traffic pattern) Prior probability of attack P(unusual traffic | attack) P(unusual traffic | no attack)
Fraud Detection (Banking / eSewa) P(Fraud | transaction at midnight from abroad) Prior fraud rate P(midnight foreign | fraud) P(midnight foreign | genuine)
Medical Diagnosis AI P(Disease | positive test) Prior disease prevalence Test sensitivity False positive rate
Search Engine Ranking P(Relevant | clicked link) Prior relevance P(click | relevant) P(click | irrelevant)
Spam filter example (classic Bayes application):
P(Spam) = 0.4 (40% of emails are spam)
P(Word “win” | Spam) = 0.3
P(Word “win” | Not Spam) = 0.001
P(Spam | “win”) = [0.3 × 0.4] / [0.3 × 0.4 + 0.001 × 0.6] ≈ 0.995 (99.5% spam)
Bayes’ theorem is the mathematical foundation of:
• Naïve Bayes Classifier (very popular in machine learning)
• Spam filters (Gmail, Outlook)
• Medical diagnosis support systems
• Fraud detection in Nepali digital wallets (eSewa, Khalti, IME Pay)

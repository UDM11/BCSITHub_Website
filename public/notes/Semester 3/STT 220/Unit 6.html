6.1 Sampling Distribution

A sampling distribution is the probability distribution of a statistic (e.g., sample mean, sample proportion) when we take many random samples of the same size from a population. It describes how the statistic varies from sample to sample.

Key Ideas

  • Statistic: A number computed from sample data (e.g., sample mean x̄, sample proportion p̂).
  • Sampling distribution of the statistic: The distribution of all possible values of that statistic over all possible samples of size n from the population.
  • Standard error (SE): The standard deviation of the sampling distribution. It measures how much the statistic typically varies from sample to sample.

Sampling Distribution of the Sample Mean (x̄)

If we take random samples of size n from a population with mean μ and standard deviation σ:

  • The mean of the sampling distribution of x̄ equals the population mean: μx̄ = μ.
  • The standard deviation of the sampling distribution (standard error of the mean) is:
SE(x̄) = σ / √n

If the population is normally distributed, the sampling distribution of x̄ is also normal. If n is large (e.g., n ≥ 30), the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) says the sampling distribution of x̄ is approximately normal regardless of the population shape.

Sampling Distribution of the Sample Proportion (p̂)

For a proportion p in the population, the sample proportion p̂ from samples of size n has:

  • Mean: μp̂ = p
  • Standard error: SE(p̂) = √[p(1−p) / n]

For large n (with np ≥ 5 and n(1−p) ≥ 5), the sampling distribution of p̂ is approximately normal.

Why it matters: Sampling distributions are the foundation of confidence intervals and hypothesis testing. We use the standard error to quantify uncertainty in our estimates.

6.2 Parameters, Statistic, Point Estimation and Interval Estimation

Parameters vs Statistic

Term Definition Example
Parameter A numerical characteristic of the population (usually unknown). Population mean μ, population proportion p, population standard deviation σ.
Statistic A numerical characteristic of a sample; used to estimate the parameter. Sample mean x̄, sample proportion p̂, sample standard deviation s.

Point Estimation

Point estimation uses a single value (a statistic) from the sample to estimate the population parameter. The statistic used is called a point estimator.

  • Point estimate of population mean μ → sample mean x̄
  • Point estimate of population proportion p → sample proportion p̂
  • Point estimate of population standard deviation σ → sample standard deviation s

Desirable properties of a good estimator: unbiasedness (mean of estimator equals parameter), consistency (gets closer to parameter as n increases), and efficiency (smaller variance is better).

Interval Estimation (Confidence Interval)

Interval estimation gives a range (interval) of values within which the population parameter is likely to fall, with a specified level of confidence (e.g., 95%). This range is called a confidence interval (CI).

Confidence interval for population mean μ (when population is normal or n is large, and σ known):

x̄ ± zα/2 · (σ / √n)

When σ is unknown, use sample standard deviation s and the t-distribution with (n−1) degrees of freedom:

x̄ ± tα/2, n−1 · (s / √n)

Confidence interval for population proportion p (large n):

p̂ ± zα/2 · √[p̂(1−p̂) / n]

Here, zα/2 (or tα/2) is the critical value for the chosen confidence level (e.g., 1.96 for 95% when using z).

Interpretation: “We are 95% confident that the true population mean (or proportion) lies within this interval.” The 95% refers to the long-run proportion of such intervals that would contain the parameter if we repeated the process many times.

6.3 Sample Size Determination

Sample size determination answers: How large should n be so that our estimate has a desired margin of error (E) at a given confidence level?

Sample Size for Estimating Population Mean μ

Margin of error for the mean: E = zα/2 · (σ / √n). Solving for n:

n = (zα/2 · σ / E)²

If σ is unknown, use a pilot study or prior estimate for σ, or use a conservative (larger) value of σ.

Sample Size for Estimating Population Proportion p

Margin of error for the proportion: E = zα/2 · √[p(1−p) / n]. Solving for n:

n = p(1−p) · (zα/2 / E)²

If p is unknown, use p = 0.5 for the most conservative (largest) sample size, since p(1−p) is maximum at p = 0.5.

Example (proportion): We want to estimate the proportion of users who prefer a new feature with margin of error E = 0.05 at 95% confidence. No prior estimate of p. Using p = 0.5 and z0.025 ≈ 1.96:

n = 0.5 × 0.5 × (1.96 / 0.05)² = 0.25 × 1536.64 ≈ 385.
So we need at least 385 respondents.
Summary: Larger sample size → smaller margin of error and narrower confidence interval. Higher confidence (e.g., 99% vs 95%) requires a larger n for the same margin of error.

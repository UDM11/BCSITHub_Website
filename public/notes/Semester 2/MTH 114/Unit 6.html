Unit 6: Integers and Division

6.1 Introduction

The study of integers and division forms the foundation of number theory, focusing on the properties and relationships of whole numbers (..., -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, ...). Division concepts underpin key ideas like divisibility, prime numbers, and modular arithmetic, which are essential in mathematics and applications like cryptography.

  • Integers: The set of all positive and negative whole numbers, including zero, denoted ℤ. Example: -3, 0, 7.
  • Division: For integers a and b (b ≠ 0), a divided by b yields a quotient and remainder, leading to concepts like greatest common divisor (GCD) and least common multiple (LCM).
  • Applications: Used in computer science (e.g., algorithms), cryptography (e.g., encryption), and pure mathematics (e.g., solving Diophantine equations).

6.2 Division, Primes, The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic (Statement Only)

  • Division: An integer a is divisible by b (b ≠ 0) if there exists an integer k such that a = b · k. Notation: b | a. Example: 6 | 12 since 12 = 6 · 2.
  • Primes: A prime number is a positive integer greater than 1 divisible only by 1 and itself. Example: 2, 3, 5, 7. A composite number has divisors other than 1 and itself. Example: 4, 6.
  • Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic: Every positive integer greater than 1 can be expressed as a unique product of prime numbers (up to the order of factors). Example: 12 = 2² · 3, 100 = 2² · 5².

6.3 The Infinitude of Primes

The infinitude of primes states that there are infinitely many prime numbers. This was famously proven by Euclid around 300 BCE.

  • Proof Idea (Euclid’s Proof): Assume there are finitely many primes: p₁, p₂, ..., pₙ. Construct N = p₁ · p₂ · ... · pₙ + 1. N is either prime or composite. If prime, it’s a new prime not in the list. If composite, it must be divisible by a prime, but none of p₁, p₂, ..., pₙ divide N (since N leaves a remainder of 1 when divided by any pᵢ). Thus, there must be another prime, contradicting the assumption. Hence, there are infinitely many primes.
  • Example: Let primes be 2, 3, 5. Then N = 2 · 3 · 5 + 1 = 31, which is prime. Repeating this process generates more primes indefinitely.
  • Applications: Ensures an infinite supply of primes for cryptography and number theory problems.

6.4 The Division Algorithm, GCD, and LCM

The division algorithm formalizes division of integers, and GCD and LCM are key tools for solving problems involving divisibility.

  • Division Algorithm: For integers a and b (b > 0), there exist unique integers q (quotient) and r (remainder) such that a = bq + r, where 0 ≤ r < b. Example: Divide a = 17 by b = 5. Then 17 = 5 · 3 + 2, so q = 3, r = 2.
  • Greatest Common Divisor (GCD): The largest positive integer that divides both a and b without a remainder. Found using the Euclidean algorithm: If a = bq + r, then GCD(a, b) = GCD(b, r). Example: Find GCD(48, 18). Steps: 48 = 18 · 2 + 12, 18 = 12 · 1 + 6, 12 = 6 · 2 + 0. Thus, GCD = 6.
  • Least Common Multiple (LCM): The smallest positive integer divisible by both a and b. Computed as LCM(a, b) = |a · b| / GCD(a, b). Example: For 48, 18, LCM = (48 · 18) / 6 = 144.
  • Applications: GCD is used in simplifying fractions and solving Diophantine equations; LCM is used in adding fractions and scheduling problems.

6.5 Modular Arithmetic

Modular arithmetic deals with integers “wrapped around” a modulus, focusing on remainders after division.

  • Definition: For integers a, b, and modulus n > 0, a ≡ b (mod n) if n divides a - b, i.e., a and b have the same remainder when divided by n. Example: 17 ≡ 5 (mod 12) since 17 - 5 = 12 is divisible by 12.
  • Properties:
    • Addition: If a ≡ b (mod n) and c ≡ d (mod n), then a + c ≡ b + d (mod n). Example: 7 ≡ 2 (mod 5), 4 ≡ 9 (mod 5), so 7 + 4 ≡ 2 + 9 ≡ 11 ≡ 1 (mod 5).
    • Multiplication: a · c ≡ b · d (mod n). Example: 3 ≡ 8 (mod 5), so 3 · 4 ≡ 8 · 4 ≡ 12 ≡ 2 (mod 5).
    • Exponentiation: If a ≡ b (mod n), then a^k ≡ b^k (mod n). Example: 2 ≡ 7 (mod 5), so 2³ ≡ 7³ ≡ 8 ≡ 3 (mod 5).
  • Applications: Used in computer algorithms, hash functions, and cryptography (e.g., RSA).

6.6 Application of Congruences in Cryptology

Congruences in modular arithmetic are fundamental to cryptology, particularly in encryption algorithms like RSA.

  • Congruences in Cryptography: Congruences ensure secure encoding and decoding of messages by working with modular arithmetic to obscure data. Example: In RSA, a message M is encrypted as C ≡ M^e (mod n), where e and n are public keys, and decrypted as M ≡ C^d (mod n), where d is the private key.
  • RSA Encryption Example: Choose primes p = 3, q = 11, so n = p · q = 33. Compute φ(n) = (p-1)(q-1) = 20. Choose e = 7 (coprime to 20), and find d such that e · d ≡ 1 (mod 20), so d = 3 (since 7 · 3 = 21 ≡ 1 (mod 20)). To encrypt M = 5, compute C = 5⁷ ≡ 14 (mod 33). Decrypt: C³ = 14³ ≡ 5 (mod 33).
  • Other Applications: Congruences are used in hash functions, digital signatures, and error detection (e.g., ISBN check digits).
  • Best Practices: Use large primes in RSA for security, ensure proper key management, and verify modular computations for accuracy.
